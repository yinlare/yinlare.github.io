Why is Hopper's New Auction Record Frivolous

Nov 14, 2018

I always wanted to write about Edward Hopper, but I never figured out how to start. Hopper has long been one of my favorite artists. He is somewhere between a household name and a lesser-known artist. Many people know him, but not many talk about him. Many museums collect his works, but few art historians seriously write about him. He remains a bit mysterious as those lonely figures in his paintings.

Things changed a couple of months ago when Christie’s announced that one of Hopper’s masterpieces, Chop Suey (1929), would be auctioned this November. I was excited about the news at first as Hopper’s oil paintings are rarely seen at auctions, especially a work of such significance. But then Christie’s started this huge market campaign of this work-a global tour and a big presence on both traditional and social media (sounds familiar right? Think about that record-breaking Salvador Mundi). Hopper became a marketable name all of a sudden, and of course the work is the cover lot of the auction An American Place | The Barney A. Ebsworth Collection Evening Sale.

Hopper has been over-hyped in the past few months - an artist who even barely talked about his works. I definitely have mixed feelings about all the publicity. On one hand, it does make Hopper more well-known to the general public, probably to the people who are not that familiar with American realist artists. On the other hand, I act like a kid who doesn’t want to share the favorite toy with others. Most importantly, Hopper’s paintings never tend to be showy.

I don’t remember since when seeing Hopper’s works became a pilgrimage; I try to find all his works from one museum to another. Like artists have personalities, I think so do their works. Hopper’s art never tries to advertise anything: oftentimes it delineates an anonymous person in a place that could be anywhere. And the melancholy and solitude disseminated from his art always make me contemplate. “Mindfulness” is a word that is probably overused today, yet Hoppers’ works do make me mindful - it is about learning to keep inner peace when you live in a chaotic world where solitude is always part of your life. I like to spend time staring at his paintings - it’s a therapeutic experience. However, seeing the massive publicity of Hopper’s masterpiece, I feel that an old friend is being misrepresented and misunderstood.

The auction just ended. Chop Suey was hammered at 85 million US dollars, a new auction record for the artist. Another work by Hopper, Cottage at North Truro (1938) was sold at 2.9 million US dollars, net of buyer’s premium. They both achieved financial successes.

I always consider myself “liberal” in terms of how I view the art market, maybe partly due to my banking background. Money always matters, right? But first time, I started thinking what those marketing campaigns mean to a broader audience - do they really help us learn about an artist and his or her works? Art auction is a pure business. Everything an auction house does is about promoting the artworks for sale. We are used to the frothy contemporary art market where every artist is presented as a celebrity, so it is okay. But when it comes to one of my favorites, I find it is actually NOT okay.

Chop Suey fetched almost 100 million US dollars today (approx. USD 92M with buyer’s premium). If there is a 100 million club in the art world, you might see Picasso, Warhol or Basquiat. I honestly don’t know how Hopper can fit in there.

Well, luckily the show is over; and the curtain is falling. I’m relieved.

Hopper’s most famous work, The Nighthawks. I first saw a copy of this work when I first moved to Canada for school. I was appealed to the sense of isolation and solitude in this work. When I visited Chicago in 2008, Art Institute of Chicago was staging Hopper’s retrospective. Now this painting is being loaned to Shanghai Museum for the special exhibition Pathways to Modernism American Art, 1865-1945.
