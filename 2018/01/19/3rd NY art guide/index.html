New York Art Guide

Dec 31, 2017

Many of my friends who have visited New York are intrigued to the city’s art scene. I often get the question: “where shall I go? Do you have any recommendation?” While there is always too much art to see in this city, the below guide lists the major museums and art galleries. Whether you aim for a deep dive or scratch the surface, I hope it will help.

Here you go.

Part 1: Museums



Metropolitan Museum of Art


Upper East Side

  • The Metropolitan Museum of Art
    Needless to say, top of your to-do list.

  • Frick Collection
    I am surprised that many people, including those who live in New York, have not heard of this gem. This is definitely my favorite museum in town and it’s got my favorite Rembrandt’s self-portrait. Intimate and classy, Frick is not as overwhelmingly large as the MET. If you are a big fan of Old Masters, you’ll be impressed. Best of all? No photographs are allowed.

  • Guggenheim
    Also one of the most renowned museums in the world. I suggest checking the ongoing exhibition before you go. But its architecture and permanent collection are worth visiting.

  • Met Breuer
    Part of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the MET Breuer stages special exhibitions of historically important artists year-round.

  • The Jewish Museum
    As its name indicates, the museum is dedicated to Jewish artists. It exhibits some interesting and well-curated exhibitions in a more intimate space.

  • Neue Galerie New York
    Museum for German and Austrian art from the early twentieth century. It has an amazing collection of Gustav Klimt’s works.

  • Cooper Hewitt, Smithsonian Design Museum
    For design lovers.

Midtown

  • Museum of Modern Art
    Often packed with tourists, MoMA houses some most important art in the past century. If you’re a serious art lover who wants to take some time there, check out its museum night.

Downtown

  • Whitney Museum of American Art
    Located along Hudson River, Whitney focused on American and living artists. Other than the MET and MoMA, I view it as a museum to complete the city’s institutional presentation whereas it is by no means less important. Its Biennial is pivotal in the art world. Its trendy location and close proximity to Highline are bonuses.

  • The New Museum of Contemporary Art
    New art, new ideas. Avant-garde art.

  • Rubin Museum of Art
    Focuses on art of Himalayan Asia.

Queen’s and Brooklyn

  • MoMA PS1
    As part of MoMA, PS1 presents cutting-edge contemporary art. It “distinguishes itself from other major art institutions in its progressive approach” in the way that you can interact with the most avant-garde art such as performance art, video art, etc. If you are a fan of James Turrell, don’t miss his site-specific installation Meeting.

  • MET Cloister
    As a branch of the MET, the MET Cloister is dedicated to European Medieval Art. Situated in Upper Manhattan, it overlooks Hudson River with an unparalleled view. Though located far from the city center, it is a gem of Medieval art.

  • Brooklyn Museum
    New York City’s second largest in physical size and holds an art collection with roughly 1.5 million works. It has an impressive collection of American art.

Part 2: Galleries





Galleries are very important part of New York’s dynamic art scene. They present varied and museum-quality exhibitions at zero cost. These galleries cluster in six areas in the city (listed in an essential-to-hardcore way): Chelsea, Upper East Side, Soho, Lower East Side, Williamsburg and Bushwick, Dumbo. While gallery crawl always involves intimidating amount of walking, check the current exhibitions beforehand to plan which ones you want to visit. Here are some useful links and app:

  • Major galleries by neighborhood
    https://www.nycgo.com/articles/nyc-art-gallery-guide

  • Artnet lists a large number of art events with filter function. It encompasses museum and gallery exhibitions as well as art fairs.
    http://www.artnet.com/events/new-york/

  • App: See Saw
    This app comes in handy. It not only lists and recommends art events by neighborhood, but also helps users to plan itinerary on a map. Unfortunately, it is only available on App Store.
